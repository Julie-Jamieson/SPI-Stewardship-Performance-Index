# The Five Pillars

The Stewardship Performance Index (SPI) evaluates long-term stewardship through five interconnected pillars.

Each pillar represents a distinct aspect of stewardship that contributes to a broader understanding of how infrastructure interacts with the natural environment, surrounding communities, and the systems that support them.

Although each pillar is evaluated independently, stewardship is inherently interconnected. A single project activity may influence more than one pillar simultaneously. The assessment methodology recognizes these relationships while ensuring each aspect of stewardship is evaluated on its own merits.

Together, the five pillars provide a balanced framework for evaluating stewardship throughout the lifecycle of infrastructure and development projects.

---

# Water

The Water pillar evaluates how responsibly a project manages water resources throughout its lifecycle.

This includes the conservation, efficient use, protection, reuse, and restoration of water resources while recognizing the importance of watersheds, aquatic ecosystems, and long-term water sustainability.

The Water pillar considers how projects influence both the quantity and quality of water available to surrounding communities and natural systems.

---

# Energy

The Energy pillar evaluates how efficiently and responsibly a project generates, consumes, manages, and recovers energy.

Consideration is given to efficiency, renewable energy integration, resilience, waste heat utilization, operational optimization, and the responsible management of energy resources.

The objective is to encourage long-term energy stewardship while reducing unnecessary environmental impacts.

---

# Ecology

The Ecology pillar evaluates how a project interacts with natural systems throughout its lifecycle.

This includes biodiversity, habitat protection and restoration, ecological integration, environmental resilience, pollution prevention, and the responsible management of ecosystems influenced by the project.

The Ecology pillar recognizes that healthy natural systems provide long-term value to both communities and infrastructure.

---

# Community

The Community pillar evaluates how a project contributes to the long-term wellbeing of the communities in which it operates.

This includes community relationships, education, employment, research partnerships, accessibility, public engagement, and other initiatives that create lasting local value.

Stewardship extends beyond environmental performance. Projects also influence the social and economic resilience of the communities that support them.

---

# Transparency

The Transparency pillar evaluates how stewardship information is documented, verified, and made accessible.

Transparent reporting allows communities, project operators, governments, researchers, and other stakeholders to understand stewardship performance over time through objective evidence, consistent reporting, and independent verification.

Transparency supports accountability by ensuring stewardship information remains accessible, understandable, and capable of independent review throughout the life of a project.

---

# Looking Ahead

The five pillars establish the structure of the Stewardship Performance Index.

Future versions of the SPI Standard will define the indicators, evidence requirements, assessment methodologies, and reporting expectations associated with each pillar.
