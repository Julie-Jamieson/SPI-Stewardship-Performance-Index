Stewardship Performance Index (SPI)
A framework for long-term stewardship, independent reporting, and continuous improvement.

Overview
Communities are increasingly being asked to host large infrastructure projects that promise investment, employment, and technological advancement. Whether those projects involve artificial intelligence, energy, manufacturing, transportation, or research, they all share something in common. They will influence the communities around them for decades.

During the approval process, developers present technical studies, municipalities review applications, and residents ask questions about water use, energy demand, environmental impacts, noise, traffic, and community benefit. These discussions are important, but they often focus on what a project intends to do rather than how it will perform over the long term.

The Stewardship Performance Index (SPI) was developed to explore a simple idea. Stewardship extends throughout the entire lifecycle of a project, from planning and approval through construction, operation, and eventual renewal or decommissioning. Rather than treating stewardship as a one-time requirement, SPI provides a framework for establishing expectations before approval, evaluating performance during operation, and encouraging continuous improvement over time.

SPI is founded on a simple principle: stewardship should be measurable.
Why SPI Exists
Most infrastructure projects are evaluated through planning approvals, environmental assessments, engineering reviews, and regulatory requirements. These processes are essential, but they are not intended to provide a long-term record of stewardship.

As projects evolve, ownership may change, municipal councils change, staff retire, technology advances, and community priorities shift. Commitments that were clearly understood during the approval process can become difficult to track years later.

SPI was developed to provide a common framework for discussing stewardship throughout the life of infrastructure. Rather than replacing existing regulations, SPI complements them by encouraging transparent reporting, independent assessment, continuous improvement, and long-term accountability. In doing so, it helps preserve institutional memory by creating a consistent stewardship record that remains meaningful even as ownership, leadership, and technology evolve.

Before Construction
SPI is intended to provide value before a project is ever approved.

The framework encourages communities and project proponents to discuss stewardship using a common set of principles rather than a collection of unrelated commitments. Water use, energy management, ecological restoration, community benefit, and transparency can all be considered together as part of a broader stewardship conversation.

For project proponents, this also provides an opportunity to establish clear stewardship expectations from the outset, creating a transparent foundation that can be measured consistently throughout the life of the project. For municipalities and communities, it creates a shared language for discussing stewardship before commitments become operating realities.

If stewardship commitments are incorporated into development approvals, Community Benefit Agreements, operating agreements, or similar municipal conditions, SPI provides a consistent framework that can be referenced long after construction has been completed. This creates continuity between the commitments made during the planning stage and the reporting that follows during operation.

During Operation
Construction is only the beginning of a project's relationship with its community.

Infrastructure changes over time. Equipment is upgraded, operational practices evolve, environmental conditions change, and community expectations continue to develop. A facility that performs well today may require different approaches ten years from now, while another project may steadily improve through innovation and experience.

SPI recognises that stewardship should evolve alongside the infrastructure itself. Rather than asking whether a project once met its commitments, the framework asks whether stewardship is improving throughout the life of the facility.

The Five Stewardship Pillars
Good stewardship cannot be reduced to a single measurement.

Environmental performance matters, but so do community relationships, operational transparency, and the responsible management of shared resources. The five stewardship pillars provide the structure through which stewardship is considered and reported. Together they create a balanced view of how infrastructure interacts with both people and the environment.

SPI currently explores stewardship through five interconnected pillars.

Water
Responsible water use through conservation, efficiency, reuse, restoration, and watershed awareness.

Energy
Efficient energy management, renewable integration, resilience, waste heat utilization, and responsible resource management.

Ecology
Protection and restoration of ecosystems through biodiversity, habitat improvement, environmental integration, and ecological resilience.

Community
Creating long-term local value through partnerships, education, employment, research, accessibility, and meaningful community engagement.

Transparency
Providing clear, consistent, and independently verified stewardship reporting that allows communities, operators, and municipalities to understand how stewardship changes over time.

Stewardship Over Time
SPI is intended to measure stewardship as an ongoing process.

Annual reporting creates a historical record that allows communities, operators, municipalities, researchers, investors, and future decision-makers to observe long-term trends rather than isolated moments. Individual stewardship ratings provide useful information, but the direction of change often tells the more important story.

1	92
2	89
3	84
4	81
5	86
A consistent pattern of improvement demonstrates an ongoing commitment to stewardship. A gradual decline provides an opportunity to identify concerns, discuss corrective actions, and monitor future progress before larger problems emerge.

Independent Reporting
For stewardship reporting to be meaningful, it must also be trusted.

SPI is not intended to function as a self-reporting system in which operators evaluate their own performance without independent oversight. Infrastructure operators are expected to provide operational data, supporting documentation, and other measurable information required by the framework. The stewardship assessment itself, however, should be prepared or independently verified through an agreed reporting framework by an organization trusted by both the infrastructure operator and the community.

Separating operational reporting from stewardship assessment helps ensure that the results represent more than the perspective of any single stakeholder. Communities gain confidence that stewardship is being assessed objectively, while operators benefit from an independent record that demonstrates long-term commitment and measurable progress.

The governance model for this process remains under development. The principle, however, is fundamental to SPI. Stewardship reporting should be transparent, objective, and sufficiently independent to earn the confidence of both operators and the communities they serve.

Continuous Improvement
The purpose of SPI is not to reward perfection or create another regulatory system.

Its purpose is to encourage better stewardship over time.

When stewardship improves, that progress should be visible. When performance begins to decline, the trend should become visible early enough to encourage discussion, corrective action, and measurable improvement. In this way, SPI functions as both a reporting framework and an early warning framework, helping communities and operators understand how stewardship is changing before significant problems develop.

A trusted stewardship record benefits both communities and operators. Communities gain a consistent record that extends beyond election cycles, ownership changes, and staff turnover, while operators gain an objective history of stewardship that demonstrates continuous improvement, strengthens public trust, and provides evidence of long-term environmental and community responsibility.

Relationship to Municipal Decision-Making
SPI carries no regulatory authority and does not replace planning approvals, environmental regulations, engineering standards, or municipal oversight.

Instead, it provides a common stewardship framework that may support evidence-based decision-making before construction begins and throughout the operational life of a project. By establishing a consistent approach to stewardship reporting, SPI helps preserve continuity as ownership changes, municipal leadership evolves, and projects mature over time.

Municipalities may choose to incorporate stewardship commitments into development approvals, Community Benefit Agreements, operating agreements, or other planning tools. Where that occurs, SPI reporting provides an objective record that helps future decision-makers understand how those commitments have evolved throughout the life of the project.

Relationship to TEEHC
SPI was originally developed during development of the TEEHC project, where it provides a framework for measuring long-term stewardship within environmentally integrated computing infrastructure.

Although SPI originated within TEEHC, the principles behind the framework extend beyond computing infrastructure alone. Any large project that influences the communities around it must manage resources responsibly, maintain public trust, and demonstrate stewardship over time. SPI is therefore being developed as an independent framework whose concepts may be explored wherever long-term stewardship is an important consideration.

Participation in SPI does not require participation in a TEEHC network.

Current Status
SPI remains an evolving framework.

The principles have been established, while the detailed scoring methodology, governance structure, verification processes, reporting standards, participation requirements, and technical metrics remain subjects for future development. Future work will also explore how independent reporting organizations may participate in preparing, verifying, and publishing stewardship assessments.

The current focus is establishing a clear foundation for what meaningful stewardship should include before determining exactly how the framework should be implemented.

Guiding Principle
Stewardship is not a one-time achievement. It is an ongoing relationship between infrastructure and the communities and environments it serves.

Good stewardship should not depend on memory, promises, or public relations. It should be visible, measurable, independently verified, and capable of improving over time.

SPI provides a framework for carrying stewardship commitments from proposal, through approval, into operation, creating a continuous record that helps communities, operators, and future decision-makers understand how that relationship evolves over time.
